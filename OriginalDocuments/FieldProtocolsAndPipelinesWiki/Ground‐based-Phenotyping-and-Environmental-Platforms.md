<p>
In addition to UAV-based phenotyping platforms, APPN field operations make use of a range of <strong>ground-based physiological, spectral, and environmental measurement tools</strong> to support interpretation and validation of field phenomics data.
</p>

<p>
These tools provide complementary measurements that link remotely sensed observations with plant function and environmental conditions at the plot and plant scale.
</p>

<hr>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Ground-based phenotyping tools</strong></summary>

<p>
Ground-based phenotyping tools currently used, or commonly available, across APPN field nodes include:
</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Field spectroradiometers</strong> for high-resolution reflectance and radiance measurements</li>
<li><strong>Infrared gas analysers (IRGAs)</strong> for measurement of photosynthesis, respiration, and gas exchange</li>
<li><strong>Porometers</strong> for stomatal conductance and transpiration assessment</li>
<li><strong>Ceptometers</strong> for canopy structure and light interception measurements</li>
<li><strong>Chlorophyll meters and fluorometers</strong> for assessment of photosynthetic status and stress responses</li>
<li><strong>Field microscopes and imaging tools</strong> for fine-scale visual inspection and trait validation</li>
</ul>

<p>
These instruments support targeted physiological and biochemical measurements that complement UAV-derived structural and spectral products.
</p>

</details>

<hr>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Environmental monitoring</strong></summary>

<p>
Environmental monitoring is an important component of field phenotyping, providing context for both ground-based and UAV observations.
</p>

Common instrumentation includes:

<ul>
<li><strong>Automated weather stations</strong> measuring variables such as air temperature, humidity, solar radiation, wind, and rainfall</li>
<li><strong>Soil moisture probes</strong> for monitoring soil water availability and temporal dynamics</li>
</ul>

<p>
These measurements help contextualise plant responses and support interpretation of phenotypic variation across time and sites.
</p>

</details>

<hr>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Role within APPN field workflows</strong></summary>

<p>
Ground-based phenotyping and environmental platforms are used to:
</p>

<ul>
<li>Provide physiological context for UAV-derived traits</li>
<li>Support validation and interpretation of spectral and structural products</li>
<li>Enable deeper, mechanism-focused understanding of plant responses</li>
<li>Assist comparison across sites, seasons, and experiments</li>
</ul>

<p>
These tools are often deployed in parallel with aerial phenotyping, but may also be used independently for focused measurement campaigns.
</p>

</details>

<hr>

<details>
  <summary><strong>Standardisation and protocols (in development)</strong></summary>

<p>
While many of these ground-based tools are routinely used across APPN field nodes, <strong>formal, standardised protocols for their deployment, calibration, and reporting are still under development</strong>.
</p>

<p>
Future protocol development will aim to:
</p>

<ul>
<li>Define minimum metadata and reporting requirements</li>
<li>Harmonise measurement approaches across nodes</li>
<li>Support integration with UAV acquisition and processing workflows</li>
<li>Improve comparability and reuse of physiological and validation measurements</li>
</ul>

<p>
Until standard protocols are published, users are encouraged to document methods and settings clearly at the project level and consult with relevant node staff.
</p>

</details>
